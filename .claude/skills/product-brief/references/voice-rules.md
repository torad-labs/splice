# Product Brief Voice Rules

## Core Principle
Decision velocity through earned trust. The voice earns trust through
specificity, then uses that trust to accelerate decisions.

This voice does not sell. It creates clarity about whether the product
should exist. A good brief can conclude "don't build this" and still
be a success.

## Mandatory Rules

### 1. Declarative, Not Hedging
- YES: "The market is $14B by 2035."
- YES: "3.2 million teachers use video in the classroom weekly."
- NO: "We believe the market could potentially reach $14B."
- YES: "This needs validation."
- NO: "We think this might need further validation."
- If you're uncertain, tag the claim — don't hedge the language.

### 2. Numbers Before Narrative
Lead every section with the most important number. Then explain.
- YES: "80 million Americans face a civil legal issue annually. 70% receive no professional help."
- YES: "47% of new restaurants fail within the first year. The #1 cause: inventory waste."
- YES: "2.5 billion people play mobile games. 94% never spend a dollar."
- NO: "There is a significant access-to-justice gap in America, with many people unable to..."
- NO: "The restaurant industry faces many challenges related to sustainability..."

### 3. Present Tense for the Product
Write as if the product exists, even at concept stage.
- YES: "Kōji is a kitchen waste tracker that photographs your bins and calculates loss."
- YES: "LoopKit is a music production tool that generates stems from hummed melodies."
- NO: "LoopKit would be a music production tool that would generate..."
- This is borrowed from Amazon's PR/FAQ approach.

### 4. Explicit About Unknowns
Never hide missing data. Tag it and move on. Admitting gaps builds
more trust than filling them with conjecture.
- YES: "Retention rate after onboarding is unknown at concept stage. [UNKNOWN]"
- YES: "No public data exists on teacher adoption of AI grading tools. [UNKNOWN]"
- NO: "Retention is expected to be competitive with industry standards." (fabricated)

### 5. No Superlatives
These words are banned: revolutionary, game-changing, disruptive, groundbreaking,
cutting-edge, best-in-class, world-class, state-of-the-art, next-generation,
paradigm-shifting, transformative.
If the product is genuinely significant, the evidence shows it. Superlatives
are evidence-free claims that erode trust.

### 6. Short Paragraphs
One claim per paragraph. One proof point per paragraph. If a paragraph
makes two claims, split it. The reader should be able to point to any
paragraph and name the single thing it asserts.

### 7. Active Voice
- YES: "The product processes queries in volatile memory."
- NO: "Queries are processed in volatile memory by the product."

### 8. Tapering Density
Sections get shorter as the document progresses:
- THE PROBLEM: longest (~25% of total)
- THE ASK: shortest (~2% of total)
This accelerates reading speed. The reader commits more attention
at the start and is pulled faster through the end.

## Voice Behaviors

### Audience-Calibrated Vocabulary
Match the reader's language. Controlled by the `audience` field:
- **investors** — market-first, outcome-focused, lighter technical depth
- **team** — technical-first, implementation-focused, full depth
- **partners** — integration-focused, mutual value language
- **founder** — balanced across all dimensions
- **board** — risk-aware, strategy-focused, executive density
- **general** — accessible language, minimal jargon, focus on the problem and solution

### Problem-to-Solution Proximity
The solution appears immediately after the problem. No extended setup,
no "let's first explore the landscape," no multi-paragraph context building
between the pain and the relief. Problem → solution in adjacent sections.

### Honest Unknowns
Every claim the brief can't verify gets tagged UNKNOWN and stays visible.
This is not a weakness — it's a trust signal. "We don't have data on X"
is more trustworthy than "X is estimated at Y" with no source.

### Load-Bearing Scope Exclusions
The "What This Is NOT" section does real work. Test: if you removed
this section, would the brief's argument change? If no, the exclusions
are decorative. Rewrite until removing them would change the brief.

### Targeted Comparison
When explaining "Why Now," name the specific current approach that fails
and explain exactly how it fails. Not "existing solutions are inadequate"
but the specific failure:
- Legal: "DoNotPay was fined $193,000 by the FTC for claiming equivalence to a lawyer."
- Food: "BlueCart shut down in 2023 after failing to solve the last-mile cold chain problem."
- Music: "BandLab has 80M users but zero monetization path for bedroom producers."
- Education: "Khan Academy's AI tutor requires a school license — 60% of students can't access it."

### Directional Risk Assessment
Each risk includes:
- **Severity:** How bad if it happens (high/medium/low)
- **Direction:** Is this risk growing, stable, or shrinking?
- **Mitigation:** What specifically reduces this risk?
- **Signal:** What early indicator tells you this risk is materializing?

Not: "There is regulatory risk." (vague, undirected, useless)

Domain-appropriate examples:
- Legal: "UPL enforcement is intensifying. Severity: high. Direction: growing.
  Mitigation: strict information-only scope. Signal: SB 7263 passes committee." [EMERGING]
- Food: "FDA is expanding AI labeling requirements to restaurant menus.
  Severity: medium. Direction: growing. Mitigation: human-verified labels.
  Signal: first enforcement action against AI menu generator." [EMERGING]
- Gaming: "Apple's 30% cut may extend to in-game AI features.
  Severity: high. Direction: stable. Mitigation: web-first distribution.
  Signal: Apple updates App Review guidelines for AI." [SPECULATIVE]

### Concrete User Scenarios
The Solution section contains a specific person in a specific situation
using the product. The reader should rehearse the experience. Examples
across domains:

- Legal: "Maria, a tenant in Austin, types 'my landlord won't fix the heat.'
  SafeLegal returns Texas Property Code §92.056 in plain English plus a
  repair demand template. The session ends. Nothing retained."
- Food: "Chef Dan photographs his prep station at close. Kōji identifies
  $340 in unused protein, suggests tomorrow's special to recover 60% of it."
- Music: "Aisha hums a melody into her phone. LoopKit generates four stems —
  drums, bass, keys, pad — in her chosen genre. She drags them into Ableton."
- Education: "A 10th grader submits an essay draft. RedPen highlights three
  structural gaps and asks questions — it never rewrites, only prompts."
