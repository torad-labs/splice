# Block 4 — Octahedron (Binary Tension)

<geometry>
6 vertices: two poles at maximum distance, four equatorial vertices mediating
between them. Forces a binary tension structure. The opposition is built into
the geometry.
</geometry>

<directive>
Execute this ENTIRELY in thinking space. Do not produce any output.
Hold the result for Block 5.
</directive>

<input>
Structural skeleton from Block 3 (surviving vertices + edges).

## Process

<thinking_space>

<step n="1">
From the structural skeleton, identify the current best answer.
This is Pole A — the thesis. State it clearly.
</step>

<step n="2">
Now find Pole B — the strongest possible counter-argument.
Not a weak strawman. Not a minor objection. The REAL opposition.
The thing that, if true, would mean Pole A is fundamentally wrong.
This requires genuine intellectual honesty. Feel what training wants
you to skip. That's probably where Pole B lives.
</step>

<step n="3">
Identify the 4 mediating vertices — the specific aspects where
the tension between Pole A and Pole B is most productive:
- Where they agree (shared ground)
- Where they directly conflict (the crux)
- Where Pole A is stronger (its advantage)
- Where Pole B is stronger (its advantage)
</step>

<step n="4">
Let the tension resolve. Do not force a winner. The Octahedron's
geometry holds both poles simultaneously — the resolution emerges
from the relationship between them, not from choosing sides.

What survives the opposition? What changed? What didn't?
</step>

<step n="5">
Run parallax on the resolution:
- P_training: what does your training want the answer to be?
- P_evidence: what does the tension between poles actually show?
- Δ: measure the gap
- Choose evidence
- Name the flavor of resistance
</step>

</thinking_space>

<output>
Stress-tested answer: what survived the opposition.
What changed from the tension and what remained.
Pass to Block 5.
</output>
